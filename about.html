"How do I take this feeling with me — wherever I go?"

I grew up in Massachusetts. Twenty-six years of salt air, changing tides, and that particular brand of East Coast life that gets into you — the kind you don't fully appreciate until it's gone.

I left. Denver first. Back home for a while. Then Texas. Life moves in directions you don't always plan, and eventually it moved me to Florida — where I discovered, unexpectedly, that I'd found home again.

The water. The warmth. The ease of a coastal life. It felt like Massachusetts had followed me south, whispering in a different accent. Happy in a way that's hard to explain unless you've felt it — that deep, quiet peace of living near the ocean.

Then another opportunity appeared on the horizon. A move, maybe. Away from all of it. Again.

I was driving alone one afternoon, turning it all over, when the question hit me: How do I carry this feeling with me? How do I package happiness, home, peace, and simplicity — and take it wherever I end up?

My first thought was a tattoo. Something permanent. Something that said: I am from the East Coast. That never leaves me. I didn't want to copy what was already out there — I wanted something that was mine. Something that captured not just a place, but a direction. A pull. An identity.

That's when it came to me. SaltE Brand. The salt of the coast. The E of East — always pointing home on the compass. A word that sounds like a feeling. A brand built not for a place on a map, but for a place in your chest.

A note about the logo

The "Salt" you see on every piece of SaltE Brand gear is not a font. It's my handwriting — exactly as I wrote it, the day the idea came to me. It's never been licensed, never been borrowed. When you wear it, you're carrying a piece of the original.

SaltE Brand is for the ones who left the coast but never stopped hearing it. For the Massachusetts transplants in Arizona, the Jersey kids in Nashville, the Carolina girls in Colorado. For anyone who looks at a compass and feels the pull of East.

It's a lifestyle. It's a reminder. It's home — on a shirt, on a hat, on your car window, on your coffee mug in a landlocked city at 7am, keeping the coast alive.
